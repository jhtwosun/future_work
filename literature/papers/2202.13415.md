## Abstract (verbatim)
Conformal prediction is a popular, modern technique for providing valid predictive inference for arbitrary machine learning models. Its validity relies on the assumptions of exchangeability of the data, and symmetry of the given model fitting algorithm as a function of the data. However, exchangeability is often violated when predictive models are deployed in practice. For example, if the data distribution drifts over time, then the data points are no longer exchangeable; moreover, in such settings, we might want to use a nonsymmetric algorithm that treats recent observations as more relevant. This paper generalizes conformal prediction to deal with both aspects: we employ weighted quantiles to introduce robustness against distribution drift, and design a new randomization technique to allow for algorithms that do not treat data points symmetrically. Our new methods are provably robust, with substantially less loss of coverage when exchangeability is violated due to distribution drift or other challenging features of real data, while also achieving the same coverage guarantees as existing conformal prediction methods if the data points are in fact exchangeable. We demonstrate the practical utility of these new tools with simulations and real-data experiments on electricity and election forecasting.

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)
Real deployments routinely violate both pillars of classical CP: data are not exchangeable (drift, autocorrelation), and the model-fitting algorithm is not symmetric (recency-weighted training, online updates). nexCP attaches user-chosen *fixed* weights w_1, ..., w_n to calibration points and a randomization-based fix for asymmetric algorithms. The paper proves a coverage guarantee that decomposes into the nominal 1 - alpha minus a *coverage gap* term equal to the total-variation distance between the joint data distribution and its swap, weighted by the chosen weights; if the data are actually exchangeable the gap is zero and classical CP is recovered, while under drift the gap is bounded and small for sensible weight choices. Crucially, weights are *fixed in advance* (unlike Tibshirani-2019 where they are likelihood ratios), so nexCP applies even when the shift is unmodeled; it is the standard tool for citing a coverage-gap bound under unknown non-exchangeability.

## Method / theorem statements
- **Setup**: nonexchangeable Z_1, ..., Z_{n+1} = (X_i, Y_i); user-supplied non-negative weights w_1, ..., w_n that sum to at most 1 (and remaining mass on the test point).
- **nexCP construction**: prediction set C(X_{n+1}) = { y : s(X_{n+1}, y) <= Quantile_{1-alpha} ( sum_{i=1}^n tilde_w_i delta_{s(X_i, Y_i)} + tilde_w_{n+1} delta_{+infty} ) }, with tilde_w_i = w_i / (1 + sum_j w_j).
- **Theorem 2 (the coverage-gap bound we rely on)**: P(Y_{n+1} not in C(X_{n+1})) <= alpha + sum_{i=1}^n tilde_w_i * d_TV( L(Z_1, ..., Z_{n+1}), L(Z_1, ..., Z_i swapped with Z_{n+1}, ..., Z_{n+1}) ). The right-hand correction is the *coverage gap*: zero under exchangeability, otherwise upper-bounded by total-variation between the joint and its i-th swap.
- **Assumptions**: weights fixed before seeing the data (no data-dependent weights without extra randomization); bounded weight sums; standard measurability.
- **Asymmetric-algorithm fix**: a randomization step (random permutation or de-randomized variant) restores the same coverage-gap bound when the score function is not invariant to data ordering.
- **Slack term**: the coverage-gap term sum_i tilde_w_i d_TV (additive, distribution-free upper bound).

## Reported results
- Electricity forecasting (drift over hours/days): nexCP keeps coverage near 1 - alpha while classical CP drops 5-15 points under drift (see paper for figures).
- US election forecasting: similar gain in coverage stability under temporal shift (see paper for details).
- Simulations confirm that the coverage gap matches the proven d_TV bound and that exchangeable data incur zero gap.

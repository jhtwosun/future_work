## Abstract (verbatim)

> In recent years, large language models have greatly improved in their ability to perform complex multi-step reasoning. However, even state-of-the-art models still regularly produce logical mistakes. To train more reliable models, we can turn either to outcome supervision, which provides feedback for a final result, or process supervision, which provides feedback for each intermediate reasoning step. Given the importance of training reliable models, and given the high cost of human feedback, it is important to carefully compare the both methods. Recent work has already begun this comparison, but many questions still remain. We conduct our own investigation, finding that process supervision significantly outperforms outcome supervision for training models to solve problems from the challenging MATH dataset. Our process-supervised model solves 78% of problems from a representative subset of the MATH test set. Additionally, we show that active learning significantly improves the efficacy of process supervision. To support related research, we also release PRM800K, the complete dataset of 800,000 step-level human feedback labels used to train our best reward model.

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

The paper makes the headline empirical claim that *process supervision* — labeling every step in a chain of thought as positive / negative / neutral — beats *outcome supervision* (labeling only the final answer) for training a reward model that picks the right MATH solution out of many sampled candidates.
The authors fine-tune a GPT-4-class base model with MathMix (1.5B math-relevant tokens) and then train a process reward model (PRM) on human step-level labels collected in two phases: phase 1 uniformly samples generator solutions, phase 2 uses active learning to oversample the highest-scoring *wrong-answer* traces ("convincing-wrong"), which gives ≈ 2.6× sample efficiency.
The released **PRM800K** dataset (800K step labels over 75K solutions to 12K problems, drawn from the MATH training set) becomes the de-facto open calibration source for downstream PRM work, including this project.
Best-of-N selection with the PRM reaches 78.2% on a 500-problem MATH test subset, well above ORM (72.4%) and majority-vote (69.6%) baselines on the same generator, and the gap widens monotonically with $N$ — a key sign that PRM signal is real and not a fixed bias.
Crucially for CoT-CP: per-step probabilities are sigmoid outputs (not calibrated), and the trace score is the product of per-step probabilities, an aggregator that lets us cleanly separate per-step calibration from trace-level coverage.

## Method
- **What is computed at each step**:
  A per-step probability $p_t = \sigma(z_t) \in (0,1)$ that the current step is *correct* (positive class).
  The PRM is a transformer with a regression head trained via cross-entropy on the human positive / negative labels.
  Neutrals (steps that are correct but irrelevant to the solution) are dropped or relabeled as positive in ablations.
  The PRM scores each prefix exactly once at decoding-completion time — there is no autoregressive interaction between PRM steps.
- **Training data**:
  - PRM800K = **800,000 step-level labels** on **75,000 solutions** to **12,000 problems** drawn from the MATH training set.
  - Phase 1: uniform sampling of generator solutions, fully labeled.
  - Phase 2: active learning. The current PRM scores many candidate solutions, and the highest-scoring *wrong-answer* traces ("convincing-wrong") are sent to human labelers, giving ≈ 2.6× sample efficiency.
  - The 500-problem MATH subset used for evaluation is held out from PRM training.
- **Score function definition**:
  For a trace $S = s_{1:T}$, the per-step score is $p_t = \mathrm{PRM}(\text{prefix}_{1:t})$.
  The trace-level score used for best-of-N re-ranking is the **product of per-step probabilities**:
  $$\mathrm{score}(S) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_t \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \log\mathrm{score}(S) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_t.$$
  Min-aggregation $\min_t p_t$ is reported in ablations and is roughly equivalent — both penalize any single low-confidence step.
- **Aggregation to final**:
  Generator samples $N$ candidate traces; PRM scores each; the argmax-score trace is returned. Best-of-**1860** is used in the headline number; ablations sweep $N \in \{1, 4, 16, 64, 256, 1860\}$.

## Reported results
- **Headline**: 78.2% on the 500-problem MATH representative subset with PRM-best-of-1860.
  - Beats ORM-best-of-1860 (72.4%) by **+5.8 absolute**.
  - Beats majority-vote-of-1860 (69.6%) by **+8.6 absolute**.
  - Same generator (GPT-4 fine-tuned on MathMix, 1.5B math-relevant tokens) for all three methods.
- **Compute scaling**: gap between PRM and ORM grows monotonically as $N$ increases — process supervision *improves* with more compute, outcome supervision plateaus near 72%.
- **Active-learning efficiency**: 2.6× sample efficiency from phase-2 (active learning on convincing-wrong) over phase-1 (uniform sampling).
- **Out-of-distribution transfer**: best-of-N gain transfers without retraining to recent AP Calculus, AP Chemistry, AP Physics, AMC10, AMC12 exams.
- **Ablations**: large vs small PRM (large helps), MathMix pretraining vs base (large effect), active learning (2.6×), neutral handling (no significant impact), generator strength (gain scales with stronger generator).
- **Downstream community confirmation** (per ProcessBench, §B4): a Qwen2.5-Math-7B PRM fine-tuned on PRM800K reaches 56.5 average F1, outperforming every other open released PRM (Math-Shepherd, RLHFlow, Skywork) by 14+ F1 points on average — PRM800K remains the highest-quality open process-supervision dataset to date.

## How CoT-CP uses this paper
- **Role**: **Calibration source** + candidate $s_t$ + headline baseline.
- **Specific use**:
  - PRM800K is the **single calibration dataset** for CoT-CP at the MVP stage. Each (problem, trace, step labels) tuple becomes a calibration example $(X_i, Y_i, \{s_t^{(i)}\}_{t=1}^{T_i})$ where $Y_i$ is final-answer correctness and $s_t^{(i)}$ is the score of step $t$.
  - The Lightman PRM's per-step probability $p_t$ is one candidate **conformity score** $s_t = 1 - p_t$ (or $-\log p_t$). Trace-level non-conformity = product / min over $t$.
  - The 78.2% MATH-500 number is a baseline; CoT-CP must demonstrate calibrated coverage *while not regressing* below this accuracy.
  - We do **not** retrain the PRM; we treat its outputs as the score on calibration data and apply CRC.
  - The phase-1/phase-2 split (uniform vs active learning) gives a natural exchangeability stress-test: calibrate on phase 1, evaluate on phase 2, watch coverage.
- **What CoT-CP adds on top**:
  - Lightman's PRM gives a *point estimate* of step correctness with no coverage guarantee — empirically known to be miscalibrated and to fail OOD (cf. ProcessBench, §B4: Math-Shepherd-PRM-7B drops from 47.9 GSM8K F1 to 23.8 Omni-MATH F1).
  - CoT-CP converts $p_t$ into a calibrated quantile $\hat{q}$ via Conformal Risk Control on PRM800K, yielding $P(\text{final answer correct}) \geq 1-\alpha$ under exchangeability.
  - Step-level branching/refinement is triggered by $s_t > \hat{q}$ — adaptive compute as a *consequence* of the calibration, not a heuristic.
  - Concretely, an MVP CoT-CP procedure: (i) score every step with Lightman-PRM on PRM800K calibration set; (ii) compute per-trace non-conformity $\max_t(1 - p_t)$; (iii) pick $\hat{q}$ as the $\lceil (n+1)(1-\alpha) \rceil$-th order statistic; (iv) at test time, abstain (or branch) on any step with $1 - p_t > \hat{q}$.
- **Required citation**: §3 (PRM800K construction), §4 (PRM training and best-of-N selection), §5.1 (active learning), Appendix (dataset stats table). This is the most-cited source in CoT-CP after Angelopoulos+ CRC.

## Limitations (what this paper does *not* deliver)
- No coverage / risk guarantee on PRM scores — purely empirical accuracy claims.
- Calibration of $p_t$ is not analyzed (sigmoid output is not a calibrated probability in general).
- No public release of the PRM weights — only the dataset PRM800K. Users must train their own PRM (community fine-tunes on Qwen2.5-Math-7B and DeepSeek-Math-7B exist).
- Distribution: GPT-4-generated MATH-style traces. Coverage to AIME 2024/2025 / Olympiad-level problems is ungrounded.
- Step segmentation is by GPT-4 generator's natural newline boundaries; no canonical step granularity is defined.

## Open questions for CoT-CP design
1. Is the per-step probability $p_t$ exchangeable across PRM800K problems? (Required for vanilla CRC.)
   - If not, weighted-CRC or domain-conditional calibration may be needed.
   - Sub-question: are GPT-4-generated traces (which dominate PRM800K) exchangeable with traces from our chosen base model (likely QwQ-32B or Qwen3-Thinking)?
2. Is the product-aggregation $\prod p_t$ the right *trace-level* non-conformity?
   - $\min_t (1 - p_t)$ aligns with "earliest error" semantics in ProcessBench.
   - $-\sum_t \log p_t$ aligns with the original Lightman trace score and preserves additivity.
3. Should we restrict calibration to phase-1 traces (uniform) and treat phase-2 (active-learned convincing-wrong) as a held-out stress test for distribution shift?
4. PRM800K labels are *human* — they reflect a specific labeling protocol. Math-Shepherd labels (auto, MC) are noisy but cheap; OmegaPRM labels (auto, MCTS) are richer. Does the choice of calibration source change the calibrated quantile $\hat{q}$ in a meaningful way?

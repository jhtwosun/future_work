## Abstract (verbatim)

"We develop new conformal inference methods for obtaining validity guarantees on the output of large language models (LLMs). Prior work in conformal language modeling identifies a subset of the text that satisfies a high-probability guarantee of correctness. These methods work by filtering claims from the LLM's original response if a scoring function evaluated on the claim fails to exceed a threshold calibrated via split conformal prediction. Existing methods in this area suffer from two deficiencies. First, the guarantee stated is not conditionally valid. The trustworthiness of the filtering step may vary based on the topic of the response. Second, because the scoring function is imperfect, the filtering step can remove many valuable and accurate claims. We address both of these challenges via two new conformal methods. First, we generalize the conditional conformal procedure of Gibbs et al. (2023) in order to adaptively issue weaker guarantees when they are required to preserve the utility of the output. Second, we show how to systematically improve the quality of the scoring function via a novel algorithm for differentiating through the conditional conformal procedure. We demonstrate the efficacy of our approach on biography and medical question-answering datasets."

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

Cherian, Gibbs and Candès extend Mohri-Hashimoto's claim-filter framework along two axes. First, they replace marginal split CP with the Gibbs-Candès *conditional* conformal procedure so the coverage guarantee can be made topic-conditional, but with a backoff that *adaptively weakens* the guarantee in regions where strict conditional validity would over-prune useful claims (utility-preserving relaxation). Second, they make the per-claim scoring function trainable by *differentiating through* the conditional conformal pipeline, so an upstream scorer can be optimized end-to-end against the (set-size, miscoverage) trade-off. The result is a tighter prediction set on biographical and medical QA at the same nominal coverage. This paper is the canonical "do better than Mohri-Hashimoto" reference; CoT-CP must position relative to it.

## Method
- **Conformity score**: a per-claim correctness scoring function s(X, claim); critical innovation is that s is *trained* by differentiating through the conditional conformal threshold computation.
- **Calibration set**: split-CP holdout, but evaluated through a conditional conformal procedure (Gibbs et al. 2023) using prompt-conditional weights.
- **Coverage statement**: conditional validity P[claim correct | topic / context] >= 1 - alpha(context), with adaptive weakening of alpha when strict conditional coverage is infeasible — explicitly a controlled trade-off between conditional validity and retention utility.
- **Granularity**: claim-level filtering (same as Mohri-Hashimoto) but with topic-adaptive thresholds.

## Reported results
- Datasets: long-form biographies (FActScore-style) and medical QA (MedLFQA / similar).
- Models: black-box LLMs; exact list per the NeurIPS PDF.
- Demonstrates strict gains in retention rate over marginal-CP claim filtering at matched conditional coverage levels; the differentiable scoring function step further tightens prediction sets.

## How CoT-CP positions against this
- **Overlap with CoT-CP**: same problem family (CP filtering on decomposed LM output); same calibration machinery (split CP on a per-unit score). Their differentiable score idea overlaps in spirit with the closest threat to CoT-CP — Differentiable Conformal Training for LLM Reasoning Factuality (arxiv 2604.20098 in our notes), and is also relevant to PRM-style scorers we use.
- **What CoT-CP adds**:
  - Cherian et al. operate at *atomic-claim* level on biographies / medical QA; CoT-CP operates at *reasoning-step* level on math reasoning. Different unit of decomposition with different correctness ground-truth (final-answer correctness, not per-claim attestation).
  - We use *frozen, training-free* scorers (token log-prob, off-the-shelf PRM, self-consistency vote). No differentiation-through-CP is performed. Theorem 2 (LR+ Pareto) instead characterizes when one off-the-shelf score dominates another at a given compute budget — a complementary contribution.
  - Cherian et al. handle conditional coverage via Gibbs-Candès conditional CP; CoT-CP focuses on *distribution shift* (MATH->AIME) using empirical-PMF weighted CP for discrete scores (Theorem 3). Different statistical target (per-domain conditioning vs cal->test shift).
  - We can — and probably should — borrow their conditional procedure to test per-difficulty-bucket coverage on OlympiadBench and MMLU-Pro STEM as a future extension.
- **Required experimental baseline**: yes, but secondary. A "Cherian et al. conditional CP" baseline at the trajectory level (using prompt-difficulty buckets as conditioning variable) on MATH-500 + OlympiadBench is a natural extension; not strictly required for paper v1, but reviewers will ask if we omit it. Differentiation-through-CP as a baseline is *not* required for v1 because CoT-CP explicitly stays training-free.
- **Required citation**: Section 2 (Related Work, conditional CP for LLMs), Section 5 (Discussion of conditional / per-difficulty coverage as future work), and Section 6 (Limitations; we restrict to marginal coverage in v1).

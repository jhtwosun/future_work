## Abstract (verbatim)

> A promising approach for improving reasoning in large language models is to use process reward models (PRMs). PRMs provide feedback at each step of a multi-step reasoning trace, potentially improving credit assignment over outcome reward models (ORMs) that only provide feedback at the final step. However, collecting dense, per-step human labels is not scalable, and training PRMs from automatically-labeled data has thus far led to limited gains. To improve a base policy by running search against a PRM or using it as dense rewards for reinforcement learning (RL), we ask: "How should we design process rewards?". Our key insight is that, to be effective, the process reward for a step should measure progress: a change in the likelihood of producing a correct response in the future, before and after taking the step, corresponding to the notion of step-level advantages in RL. Crucially, this progress should be measured under a prover policy distinct from the base policy. We theoretically characterize the set of good provers and our results show that optimizing process rewards from such provers improves exploration during test-time search and online RL. In effect, our characterization shows that optimizing process rewards from a weak prover policy can substantially improve the test-time search performance compared to outcome rewards. We validate our claims by training process reward verifiers (PAVs) to predict progress under such provers and show that compared to ORMs, test-time search against PAVs is >8% more accurate, and 1.5–5× more compute-efficient. Online RL with dense rewards from PAVs enables one of the first results with 5–6× gain in sample efficiency and >6% gain in accuracy, over outcome rewards.

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

Setlur et al. argue that the *right* per-step process reward is not "is this step correct?" (Math-Shepherd) nor "is the trace correct?" (ORM) but the **step advantage** $A^\mu(s_t, a_t)$ under a deliberately *different* prover policy $\mu$.
The crucial — and counterintuitive — claim is that if you measure advantage under the *base* policy $\pi$ itself, the resulting RL gradient collapses back to the ORM update and gives no extra signal; you must use a *complementary* prover $\mu \neq \pi$.
They prove a policy-improvement lower bound that decomposes into a *distinguishability* term (variance of $A^\mu$) and an *alignment* term (correlation of $A^\mu$ and $A^\pi$), and show experimentally that small Best-of-K provers (e.g., BoK=4) on top of the base policy maximize this trade-off.
Concretely, training Process Advantage Verifiers (PAVs) to predict $A^\mu$ gives >8% accuracy and 1.5–5× compute efficiency in beam search and 5–6× sample efficiency in online RL.
For CoT-CP this paper is dual-use: $A^\mu$ is one of the strongest candidate scores, *and* PAV-driven beam search is the closest deployed-system competitor on the "adaptive compute via PRM" axis (G5 in the litreview).

## Method
- **What is computed at each step**: $A^\mu(s_t, a_t) := Q^\mu(s_t, a_t) - V^\mu(s_t)$ — the step-level advantage (i.e., progress toward correctness) measured under prover policy $\mu$.
- **Training data**: For each prefix state $s_t$ and step action $a_t$, estimate $Q^\mu$ and $V^\mu$ via Monte Carlo rollouts of $\mu$ from $(s_t, a_t)$ vs $s_t$. The "first pit" data-collection strategy targets high-value states with sub-zero rollout success and the first $Q^\pi = 0$ step in a failing trace. Total training set: ≈ 300K (prefix, $\hat{Q}^\pi$) pairs per base policy.
- **Score function definition**: $s_t = A^\mu(s_t, a_t)$. The PAV is a regression head trained on these MC-estimated advantages.
- **Why prover must differ from base** (§3, Theorem 3.1):
  1. If $\mu = \pi$, $\mathbb{E}[A^\pi \cdot \nabla \log \pi]$ reduces to the ORM gradient — no new signal.
  2. If $\pi$ is weak, $Q^\pi \approx 0$ everywhere ⇒ $A^\pi \approx 0$ ⇒ uninformative.
  3. If $\mu$ is *too strong*, $A^\mu \approx 0$ on every step (anything succeeds) ⇒ no distinguishability.
- **Good prover characterization** (Thm 3.1 lower bound):
  $$\Delta J(\pi) \gtrsim \gamma \cdot \underbrace{\mathbb{V}_{a \sim \pi_t}[A^\mu(s,a)]}_{\text{distinguishability}} \;+\; \gamma \cdot \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi_t}[A^\mu(s,a) A^{\pi_t}(s,a)]}_{\text{alignment}}.$$
  The optimal prover maximizes the sum: distinguish actions while staying aligned with $\pi$'s judgment.
- **Aggregation to final**: Effective reward used in RL: $r^{\text{eff}}_t = Q^\pi(s_t,a_t) + \alpha \cdot A^\mu(s_t,a_t)$, with $\alpha \in [0.2, 5]$ tuned per setting. Beam-search uses $\sum_t A^\mu$ as trace score.

## Reported results
- **Datasets**: MATH (Hendrycks 2021).
- **Base policies**: Gemma-2-{2B, 9B, 27B}, SFT'd on MATH.
  - Training: 5000 iterations, batch size 32, max LR: 5e-6 (2B/9B), 5e-7 (27B).
- **Prover choices** (Fig 5):
  - Bo4 of base is optimal in beam search for Gemma-2B.
  - For Gemma-{2B, 9B} base, Gemma-{9B, 27B} provers work well.
  - *Counterintuitive finding*: a 9B prover beats a 27B prover for a 27B base policy — strong provers lose distinguishability ($A^\mu \approx 0$ on most steps).
- **Test-time search** (Fig 4):
  - PAV vs ORM in beam search: **>8% accuracy gain**.
  - Compute efficiency: **1.5–5× fewer rollouts** for matched accuracy (10× for 2B/9B, 5× for 27B).
- **Online RL** (Fig 7):
  - **5–6× sample efficiency** vs ORM-RL (iterations to matched accuracy).
  - >7% accuracy gain over ORM-RL; 11% over RFT for Gemma-2B; 15% for Gemma-9B.
  - 8× better Pass@N (solution diversity).
- **Didactic planted-sub-sequence task**: 10× sample efficiency in RL with effective rewards, 5× faster Pass@N growth.
- **Hyperparameter $\alpha$**:
  - Search: $\alpha = 0.5$ for Gemma-2B / 9B, $\alpha = 0.2$ for 27B.
  - RL: $\alpha = 5.0$ for 2B, $\alpha = 3.0$ for 9B.
- **Training data**: ≈ 300K (prefix, $\hat{Q}^\pi$) pairs per base policy via "first pit" Q-estimation (target steps where $Q^\pi$ first hits 0 in failing trajectories).

## How CoT-CP uses this paper
- **Role**: **Candidate score function** + a **HIGH-threat adaptive-compute competitor** (G5 in Section G of the litreview).
- **Specific use**:
  - $A^\mu$ is one of the candidate non-conformity scores: $s_t = -A^\mu(s_t, a_t)$ (lower advantage ⇒ less progress ⇒ higher non-conformity).
  - PAVs are trained for compute-efficient *beam search* — exactly the test-time use-case CoT-CP must beat or wrap. We will report CoT-CP-on-top-of-PAV as one configuration.
  - Their compute-efficiency curve is a key reference for CoT-CP's "compute vs. risk" plots.
  - We do **not** use the prover-policy theory to design our score — CoT-CP treats $A^\mu$ as a black box.
- **What CoT-CP adds on top**:
  - PAVs improve *expected* test-time accuracy with no formal coverage guarantee on any single prompt. CoT-CP's CRC layer turns "use $A^\mu$ heuristically" into "$P(\text{answer correct}) \geq 1-\alpha$".
  - The CoT-CP gating threshold $\hat{q}$ is calibrated; PAV beam-search heuristics are tuned per base/prover pair (see Fig 5/6 sweeps in the PAV paper).
  - PAVs are still subject to OOD failure (per ProcessBench-style OOD test), and CRC's distribution-free guarantee absorbs that without retraining.
- **Required citation**: §3 (definition of progress + Thm 3.1), §4 (data collection + first-pit), §5 (test-time search results), §6 (online RL). Most-cited section: §3 for the formal definition of step reward, since CoT-CP needs to clearly distinguish "using $A^\mu$" from "calibrating $A^\mu$".

## Limitations (what this paper does *not* deliver)
- No coverage / risk guarantee — every claim is an expected-value statement.
- Restricted to MATH-distribution problems and Gemma-2 base policies; OOD evaluation (e.g., AIME, Olympiad) not reported.
- Prover-policy choice is empirically tuned per (base, dataset) pair via Fig 5 sweep — implicit per-environment hyperparameter.
- Theory (Thm 3.1) gives a *lower bound* on improvement, not an upper bound on error.
- Training cost: ≈300K labeled prefixes per base policy with MC rollouts. Math-Shepherd is cheaper at the cost of weaker supervision.

## Open questions for CoT-CP design
1. PAVs assume access to a separate prover policy $\mu$ — does CoT-CP need this, or can we calibrate any $s_t$ regardless of its semantics? Hypothesis: CRC is score-form-agnostic, so we do *not* need a prover; this is a CoT-CP simplification.
2. The "complementary prover" condition (Thm 3.1) is a *training-time* property of the PAV. Once trained, the per-step output $A^\mu$ is just a scalar — the calibration is unaffected by how it was constructed. Useful for proving CoT-CP's score-agnosticism.
3. PAV experiments are on MATH only, with Gemma-2 base policies. ProcessBench-style OOD evaluation on PAVs has not been published; CoT-CP can run that evaluation as a side contribution and use it to justify the calibration layer.

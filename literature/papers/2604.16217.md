## Abstract (verbatim)

"Large language models are increasingly deployed in settings where reliability matters, yet output-level uncertainty signals such as token probabilities, entropy, and self-consistency can become brittle under calibration--deployment mismatch. Conformal prediction provides finite-sample validity under exchangeability, but its practical usefulness depends on the quality of the nonconformity score. We propose a conformal framework for LLM question answering that uses internal representations rather than output-facing statistics: specifically, we introduce Layer-Wise Information (LI) scores, which measure how conditioning on the input reshapes predictive entropy across model depth, and use them as nonconformity scores within a standard split conformal pipeline. Across closed-ended and open-domain QA benchmarks, with the clearest gains under cross-domain shift, our method achieves a better validity--efficiency trade-off than strong text-level baselines while maintaining competitive in-domain reliability at the same nominal risk level. These results suggest that internal representations can provide more informative conformal scores when surface-level uncertainty is unstable under distribution shift."

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

The paper argues output-facing uncertainty signals (token probabilities, entropy, self-consistency) are brittle under calibration-deployment mismatch and proposes **Layer-Wise Information (LI) scores** — measurements of how conditioning on the input *reshapes predictive entropy across model depth* — as a nonconformity score for split CP. LI scores require access to internal representations (white-box LLMs only); they aggregate cross-depth information in a way that is more stable under distribution shift than logit-based scores. Plugged into standard split-CP, LI gives a better validity-efficiency trade-off than text-level baselines (token-prob, entropy, self-consistency) on QA, with the clearest wins under cross-domain shift.

## Method

- **Conformity score**: **Layer-Wise Information (LI) scores** — a function of how predictive entropy at each transformer layer changes when the input is conditioned on, aggregated across depth. The score is computed from internal hidden states / per-layer logit lenses.
- **Calibration set**: standard split-CP — a held-out QA calibration set, LI scores computed for each (input, gold-answer) pair, threshold set at the (1 - alpha) empirical quantile.
- **Coverage statement**: standard split-CP marginal coverage at 1 - alpha under exchangeability; the paper's empirical claim is that LI degrades less than surface scores when exchangeability is violated by domain shift.
- **Granularity**: **whole-answer / QA level** — one prediction set per question; not step-level.

## How CoT-CP positions against this

- **Overlap with CoT-CP**: Both target robust CP for LLMs and acknowledge that surface-level scores are unstable. Internal-representation scores are an alternative path that CoT-CP must address as related work.
- **What CoT-CP adds**:
  1. **Black-box / API compatibility.** LI scores require **internal hidden states across all layers** — fundamentally a white-box method. CoT-CP works on closed-source API LLMs (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) where LI is impossible. This is a strict capability differentiator.
  2. **Step granularity vs. answer granularity.** LI-CP is one prediction set per QA item; CoT-CP calibrates *per reasoning step*, which is finer-grained and supports per-step abstain.
  3. **Composable, not in conflict.** On open-weight LLMs CoT-CP could *use* LI scores as the per-step conformity score — this is a constructive complementary direction, not a competition.
  4. **Reasoning-chain artifacts, not single-shot QA.** LI is evaluated on QA; CoT-CP targets multi-step proof / tool-use / agent traces.
- **Required experimental baseline**: only if we run on open-weight LLMs (LLaMA-class) where internal states are accessible. On API-only experiments, citation without head-to-head suffices.
- **Required citation**: yes, **section 2 (related work, internal-representation CP subsection)** and in the discussion of conformity-score design. Also cite as motivation for "surface scores are brittle under shift" claim.

## Abstract (verbatim)

Enabling LLMs to improve their outputs by using more test-time computation is
a critical step towards building generally self-improving agents that can
operate on open-ended natural language. In this paper, we study the scaling
of inference-time computation in LLMs, with a focus on answering the
question: if an LLM is allowed to use a fixed but non-trivial amount of
inference-time compute, how much can it improve its performance on a
challenging prompt? Answering this question has implications not only on the
achievable performance of LLMs, but also on the future of LLM pretraining
and how one should tradeoff inference-time and pre-training compute. Despite
its importance, little research attempted to understand the scaling
behaviors of various test-time inference methods. Moreover, current work
largely provides negative results for a number of these strategies. In this
work, we analyze two primary mechanisms to scale test-time computation: (1)
searching against dense, process-based verifier reward models; and (2)
updating the model's distribution over a response adaptively, given the
prompt at test time. We find that in both cases, the effectiveness of
different approaches to scaling test-time compute critically varies
depending on the difficulty of the prompt. This observation motivates
applying a "compute-optimal" scaling strategy, which acts to most
effectively allocate test-time compute adaptively per prompt. Using this
compute-optimal strategy, we can improve the efficiency of test-time compute
scaling by more than 4x compared to a best-of-N baseline. Additionally, in a
FLOPs-matched evaluation, we find that on problems where a smaller base model
attains somewhat non-trivial success rates, test-time compute can be used to
outperform a 14x larger model.

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

Snell+ formalize **compute-optimal test-time scaling** as choosing the best
inference strategy θ (best-of-N vs. sequential revision vs. PRM-guided
beam/lookahead search) **per prompt q, conditional on prompt difficulty**,
to maximize expected correctness at fixed compute budget N. Difficulty is
estimated by binning the model's pass@1 rate (oracle, from 2048 samples
per question) into **five quantiles**, and the optimal strategy is shown to
flip across bins: easy prompts benefit from sequential revisions, hard
prompts from a balance of parallel sampling + verifier search. The
headline empirical claims are **>4× compute efficiency over best-of-N**
on MATH and **a smaller model + compute-optimal beats a 14× larger model**
on prompts with non-trivial baseline success. This is the **closest
spiritual ancestor of CoT-CP** — adaptive compute conditional on a
per-prompt signal — but the difficulty estimator is an *oracle proxy*
(pass@1 on 2048 samples) and the strategy selector has no coverage
guarantee, only empirical efficiency.

## Method

- **Step-level signal used**: **Prompt-level** difficulty bin, not
  step-level. Two estimators:
  1. **Oracle difficulty**: bin model's empirical pass@1 rate (computed
     from 2048 samples per question against ground truth) into 5
     quantiles.
  2. **Model-predicted difficulty**: replace the ground-truth pass@1
     with the **averaged final-answer score from a learned PRM/verifier**
     across 2048 samples — same binning, label-free proxy.
- **Stopping/branching rule (compute-optimal)**: For each compute budget N
  and ground-truth answer y*(q), the paper defines
  θ*_{q,a*(q)}(N) = argmax_θ E_{y ~ Target(θ, N, q)}[ 1{y = y*(q)} ]
  i.e. the strategy θ that maximizes expected correctness at budget N for
  prompt q. In practice, θ ranges over (i) best-of-N, (ii) sequential
  revisions, (iii) beam search against a PRM, (iv) lookahead search, plus
  hybrid mixtures (parallel + sequential split).
- **Threshold tuning**: The argmax over θ is **computed empirically on a
  validation split** of MATH per difficulty bin, then applied at test
  time conditional on the predicted difficulty bin. There is no
  finite-sample / coverage guarantee on the resulting per-prompt accuracy;
  the bin → strategy table is a learned lookup.
- **Per-prompt vs per-step adaptation**: **Per-prompt** (one strategy
  selected per question via difficulty bin). Within a strategy, the
  search/revision proceeds without per-step adaptation.

## Reported empirical results (the headline numbers)

- **Backbone**: PaLM 2-S* family fine-tunes (revision model + PRM
  verifier).
- **Benchmark**: MATH (Hendrycks).
- **Headline 1**: compute-optimal scaling **>4× more compute-efficient
  than best-of-N** at matched accuracy on MATH (Figure 1, abstract).
- **Headline 2**: in a **FLOPs-matched** comparison
  (D_inference / D_pretrain ratio R), the compute-optimal smaller model
  outperforms a **14× larger model** on questions where the smaller model
  has non-trivial baseline pass-rate. The exchange formula:
  total compute multiplier = M + 3·(D_pretrain/D_inference)·(M − 1)
  (where M is the parameter ratio).
- **Mechanistic finding**: easy prompts → sequential revisions dominate;
  hard prompts → parallel + verifier-search dominate. Strategy choice
  flips across the 5 difficulty quintiles.

## Where their threshold is heuristic (positioning)

- **Difficulty estimator is an oracle proxy** in the cleanest results:
  pass@1 over 2048 samples *with ground-truth labels*. The
  label-free PRM-based estimator is a degraded proxy; the paper
  acknowledges its accuracy gap relative to oracle binning.
- **Bin → strategy lookup is fit on validation MATH**: the table is
  empirical and there is no statistical guarantee that the chosen θ at
  test time hits a target accuracy on a new prompt. Move benchmark (MATH
  → AIME / GPQA / OlympiadBench) and the bin → strategy table must be
  refit; the >4× efficiency claim does not transfer with a guarantee.
- The "compute-optimal" framing is **maximum-expected-correctness, not
  coverage-controlled**: there is no statement of the form P(correct) ≥
  1 − α. The argmax-over-θ procedure can favor strategies that look good
  on average but undershoot on specific prompts.
- **Per-prompt difficulty estimation requires 2048 samples** in the
  oracle/PRM variants — a non-trivial overhead that the paper folds into
  the FLOPs accounting only loosely. Real deployment must use a
  one-shot difficulty predictor, which the paper does not provide a
  calibrated method for.
- The 14× FLOPs-matched comparison depends on the
  D_inference / D_pretrain ratio R; for typical deployment ratios the
  break-even shifts and the headline claim weakens.

## How CoT-CP positions against this

- **Empirical overlap**: Partial — MATH overlaps directly. AIME and
  GPQA-Diamond are natural extensions; we should reproduce Snell-style
  compute-optimal allocation on at least one of them with a modern
  reasoning model and report the comparison.
- **What CoT-CP adds**:
  - Distribution-free coverage statement P(final answer correct) ≥ 1 − α
    via CRC, where Snell+ deliver only empirical efficiency.
  - **Step-level** rather than **prompt-level** adaptation: CoT-CP gates
    at step boundaries inside a single trace, while Snell+ pick one
    trace-level strategy per prompt and run it to completion.
  - Replaces the **oracle pass@1 / 2048-sample** difficulty estimator
    with a **calibrated per-step score** that does not require ground
    truth and is available on the first sample.
  - Removes the bin → strategy lookup table: a single calibrated q̂
    drives the gating.
  - The Snell+ difficulty signal is itself usable as a *score* in CoT-CP
    (calibrated PRM-pass-rate), making CoT-CP a natural extension that
    inherits Snell's compute-efficiency arguments while adding a
    statistical guarantee.
- **Required experimental baseline**: yes — must reproduce a
  compute-optimal allocation baseline (best-of-N vs revision vs PRM-search
  per difficulty bin) on MATH-500 with a modern reasoning backbone, and
  ideally a second benchmark (AIME-24/25 or GPQA-Diamond).
- **Required citation**: §1 (Introduction — closest theoretical ancestor
  for adaptive test-time compute), §3 (Related Work — compute-optimal
  inference subsection), §5 (Experiments — baseline column where
  feasible), §6 (Discussion — Snell's prompt-level adaptation as a
  trace-level special case of CoT-CP's step-level CRC).

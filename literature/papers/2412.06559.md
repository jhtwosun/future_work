## Abstract (verbatim)

> As language models regularly make mistakes when solving math problems, automated identification of errors in the reasoning process becomes increasingly significant for their scalable oversight. In this paper, we introduce PROCESSBENCH for measuring the ability to identify erroneous steps in mathematical reasoning. It consists of 3,400 test cases, primarily focused on competition- and Olympiad-level math problems. Each test case contains a step-by-step solution with error location annotated by human experts. Models are required to identify the earliest step that contains an error, or conclude that all steps are correct. We conduct extensive evaluation on PROCESSBENCH, involving two types of models: process reward models (PRMs) and critic models, where for the latter we prompt general language models to critique each solution step by step. We draw two main observations: (1) Existing PRMs typically fail to generalize to more challenging math problems beyond GSM8K and MATH. They underperform both critic models (i.e., prompted general language models) and our own trained PRM that is straightforwardly fine-tuned on the PRM800K dataset. (2) The best open-source model, QwQ-32B-Preview, has demonstrated the critique capability competitive with the proprietary model GPT-4o, despite that it still lags behind the reasoning-specialized o1-mini. We hope PROCESSBENCH can foster future research in reasoning process assessment, paving the way toward scalable oversight of language models.

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

ProcessBench is the canonical *step-error-localization* benchmark: 3,400 expert-annotated test cases drawn from the GSM8K, MATH, OlympiadBench, and Omni-MATH test sets, with solutions generated by 12 distinct open-source LMs to ensure stylistic diversity.
The task is, given a problem and a step-segmented model-generated solution, to predict the index of the *earliest* erroneous step (or $-1$ if all are correct).
The evaluation metric is the **F1** between accuracy on erroneous samples and accuracy on correct samples (harmonic mean), penalizing both over- and under-flagging.
The headline finding is brutal: every existing open PRM (Math-Shepherd, RLHFlow, Skywork) collapses on the Olympiad subsets — Math-Shepherd-PRM-7B drops from 47.9 F1 on GSM8K to 24.8 / 23.8 on OlympiadBench / Omni-MATH — exposing severe out-of-distribution failure of PRMs trained on GSM8K/MATH-style data.
A simple PRM800K-fine-tuned Qwen2.5-Math-7B beats every released PRM by a wide margin, and o1-mini (87.9 average F1) laps every other system by a still-larger margin, suggesting that reasoning-model RL produces a stronger implicit critic than any released PRM.

## Method
- **What is computed at each step**: For evaluation: a binary correctness prediction per step (PRMs) or a paragraph index returned by a prompted critic model.
- **Training data**: ProcessBench is *only* an evaluation set. No train split.
- **Source distribution**:
  - **GSM8K**: 400 cases (207 erroneous + 193 correct).
  - **MATH**: 1,000 cases (594 erroneous + 406 correct).
  - **OlympiadBench**: 1,000 cases (661 erroneous + 339 correct).
  - **Omni-MATH**: 1,000 cases (759 erroneous + 241 correct).
  - Total: 3,400.
- **Solution generation**: 12 distinct open-source LMs (Qwen + LLaMA families across sizes) for stylistic diversity.
- **Step granularity**: Solutions reformatted via Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct to insert double-line-break separators consistently; <0.5% content drift, those drifted are dropped.
- **Annotation**: Doctoral-level human experts annotate the earliest error step. ≥3-of-5 agreement required; ≈30% discard rate from disagreements. Annotators given reference solutions but instructed to verify the model-generated solution step-by-step.
- **Process-error rate** on solutions with *correct final answers*: 3.5% GSM8K, 18.8% MATH, 32.2% OlympiadBench, 51.8% Omni-MATH. First quantitative evidence that hard problems often produce correct answers via flawed reasoning.
- **Score function definition**: F1 = harmonic mean of (accuracy on erroneous samples, accuracy on correct samples), reported per subset and averaged. A balanced design avoids degenerate "always say correct" / "always say wrong" wins.
- **Aggregation to final**: One F1 per (model, subset); paper-reported "Average" is the unweighted mean over the four subsets.

- All open PRMs (Math-Shepherd, RLHFlow, Skywork) **collapse on Olympiad-level problems**, dropping 25–35 F1 points relative to GSM8K — a near-50% relative degradation.
- The PRM800K-fine-tuned Qwen baseline beats every released PRM, suggesting *training-data quality and distribution* dominate architectural details.
- Reasoning models (QwQ, o1-mini) close most of the gap as critic models — process supervision skill apparently emerges from RL on outcome rewards.
- Process-error rate on solutions with **correct final answers**: 3.5% on GSM8K, 18.8% MATH, 32.2% OlympiadBench, 51.8% Omni-MATH — *first quantitative evidence* that on harder problems, models often arrive at correct answers via flawed reasoning. Direct rebuttal to "rule-based RL on outcomes is enough".

## How CoT-CP uses this paper
- **Role**: **Evaluation benchmark** for the step-detector / step-uncertainty component of CoT-CP.
- **Specific use**:
  - We report ProcessBench F1 for every CoT-CP score variant ($s_t$ candidates from PRM800K-PRM, Math-Shepherd, PAV, self-consistency, log-prob).
  - The Olympiad and Omni-MATH subsets become CoT-CP's **OOD calibration test** — calibrating on PRM800K-derived labels and evaluating coverage on these subsets stresses the exchangeability assumption exactly the way reviewers will demand.
  - The 32–52% process-error-with-correct-final-answer statistic motivates *why* answer-level CP is insufficient and step-level CP is necessary.
  - We do **not** train on ProcessBench; it is held out as evaluation only.
- **What CoT-CP adds on top**:
  - Existing PRMs collapse OOD with no warning to the user. CoT-CP's CRC layer reports a *coverage gap* when calibration fails — the user can see when the guarantee breaks instead of silently trusting a miscalibrated PRM.
  - We can use ProcessBench-style "earliest-error" detection as the loss inside CRC: $\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{1}[\text{first uncalibrated-OK step is actually wrong}]$ — directly aligns the calibration target with downstream evaluation.
- **Required citation**: §3 (data construction), §4 (evaluation results — Table 3), §5.2 (process-error-rate analysis on correct final answers).

## Limitations (what this paper does *not* deliver)
- Only labels the *first* error, not a fine-grained per-step correctness signal — limits use as direct calibration data.
- No "neutral / arguable" label (unlike PRM800K), so steps that are correct-but-irrelevant are forced into a binary bucket.
- No public training split — ProcessBench is exclusively for evaluation.
- F1 metric is not a coverage statement; cannot directly substitute for CRC's risk control.
- Solution diversity comes from 12 LMs but all in Qwen / LLaMA families — closed-source models like GPT-4 / Claude are absent from solution generation.

## Open questions for CoT-CP design
1. Is the calibration set (PRM800K, MATH-test problems only) exchangeable with the ProcessBench evaluation set (which includes OlympiadBench / Omni-MATH problems generated by 12 different LLMs)? Almost certainly *no* — this is the OOD test that will reveal whether CRC's exchangeability assumption holds for our score.
2. ProcessBench's F1 metric is *not* a coverage metric. We should report both (a) ProcessBench F1 (descriptive) and (b) CRC empirical coverage on a held-out ProcessBench split (calibration target).
3. The 32–52% process-error-with-correct-final-answer rate on Olympiad subsets is a *contamination* of outcome-label calibration: even an oracle outcome verifier would mark these "correct", but they contain genuine reasoning errors. Argument for step-level CP over answer-level CP.

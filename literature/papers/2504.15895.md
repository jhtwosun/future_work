## Abstract (verbatim)

Recent advances in large reasoning language models (LRLMs) rely on test-time
scaling, which extends long chain-of-thought (CoT) generation to solve complex
tasks. However, overthinking in long CoT not only slows down the efficiency of
problem solving, but also risks accuracy loss due to the extremely detailed or
redundant reasoning steps. We propose a simple yet effective method that
allows LLMs to self-truncate CoT sequences by early exit during generation.
Instead of relying on fixed heuristics, the proposed method monitors model
behavior at potential reasoning transition points and dynamically terminates
the next reasoning chain's generation when the model exhibits high confidence
in a trial answer. Our method requires no additional training and can be
seamlessly integrated into existing o1-like reasoning LLMs. Experiments on 10
reasoning benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K, MATH-500, AMC, GPQA, AIME and
LiveCodeBench) show that the proposed method is consistently effective on 11
cutting-edge reasoning LLMs of varying series and sizes, reducing the length
of CoT sequences by an average of 19.1% to 80.1% while improving accuracy by
0.3% to 5.0%.

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

DEER instruments long-form reasoning models (R1, QwQ-style "thinking" models)
by detecting **action transition tokens** ("Wait", "Alternatively", "Hmm")
that mark chunk boundaries inside the CoT. At each such transition, DEER
forces the model to emit a **trial answer** under a fixed prompt template,
measures the answer's token-averaged probability, and exits the CoT (skipping
straight to the final answer) if that confidence exceeds a threshold λ
(default λ ≈ 0.95). The paper sells itself as "instead of relying on fixed
heuristics", but the threshold is itself a fixed scalar, hand-picked from a
sweep over {0.94, 0.95, 0.96, 0.97}, and is **not** calibrated to give a
coverage guarantee — exactly the framing CoT-CP overturns.

## Method

- **Step-level signal used**: At each detected transition token, prompt the
  model with a fixed "Final Answer:" template and read off the
  **token-averaged max-softmax probability** of the produced trial answer.
  Equation 2: p(a_t) = softmax(M(P, T, I, a_<t)). Equation 3: trial-answer
  confidence is the mean over the answer-tokens of max-softmax. (Verbatim:
  "For multi-token trial answers, the overall confidence is computed as the
  mean confidence across all constituent tokens.")
- **Stopping/branching rule**: If trial-answer confidence ≥ λ, **commit** to
  that trial answer and terminate the CoT; else, continue generating the
  next reasoning chunk. The transition-monitor scans for the literal tokens
  "Wait", "Alternatively", "Hmm" (verbatim from §4.1: "transitions between
  these chunks are often marked by action transition points, including
  'Wait', 'Alternatively', and 'Hmm'").
- **Threshold tuning**: λ is a **scalar tuned by sweep**. §5.3 reports
  results "when the threshold is set to 0.94, 0.95, 0.96, and 0.97" without
  a principled selection rule. Different λ for different task families:
  programming uses λ = 0.97 (Figure 6b), reasoning uses λ ≈ 0.95, with no
  derivation.
- **Per-prompt vs per-step adaptation**: **Per-step** (decision is made at
  every transition token), but the *threshold* is **fixed across prompts**.
  No prompt-conditional calibration.

## Reported empirical results (the headline numbers)

- 11 reasoning LLMs evaluated (DeepSeek-R1-Distill family, QwQ-32B,
  Skywork-R1, etc.) across 10 benchmarks.
- **Token reduction range**: 19.1% – 80.1% (averaged over benchmarks).
- **Accuracy delta**: +0.3% to +5.0% — i.e. early-exit is *helpful*, not
  just neutral, on these models (a known overthinking phenomenon).
- Specific reported wins: MATH-500 typically -30%–50% tokens at neutral or
  improved accuracy; AIME 2024 -20%–40% tokens at neutral accuracy.
- Programming (LiveCodeBench): smaller token reduction, requires higher λ.

## Where their threshold is heuristic (positioning)

- The paper claims "instead of relying on fixed heuristics" but **λ is a
  fixed heuristic scalar**. The only "dynamism" is that it is read at every
  transition rather than at end-of-trace.
- §5.3 sweeps λ ∈ {0.94, 0.95, 0.96, 0.97} on each benchmark and picks
  whichever gives the best accuracy/efficiency trade-off. There is no
  held-out calibration; the sweep is on the eval benchmark itself.
- Per-task threshold choice (programming λ=0.97, reasoning λ=0.95) is given
  with **no statistical justification** — the paper offers only the
  observation that programming "needs more confidence."
- The trial-answer confidence itself is a **token-averaged max-softmax**
  with known calibration pathologies on long reasoning traces (drift,
  premature certainty); §6 of the paper does not characterize miscoverage
  rates under shifted distributions. If you change benchmark difficulty
  distribution or model family (e.g. R1 → o-series, Qwen3 → GPT-OSS), λ
  must be re-tuned and the implicit accuracy guarantee silently degrades.

## How CoT-CP positions against this

- **Empirical overlap**: Same benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH-500, AMC, GPQA, AIME,
  LiveCodeBench) and overlapping models (R1-distill, QwQ). DEER is a direct
  baseline.
- **What CoT-CP adds**:
  - Distribution-free coverage statement P(final answer correct) ≥ 1 − α
    via CRC, where DEER offers only an empirical λ-sweep.
  - **Explicit calibration** on a held-out PRM800K-style set, with the
    quantile q̂ replacing the sweep-tuned λ. The user picks α, not λ.
  - The **trial-answer confidence signal** from DEER is a perfectly valid
    score function for CoT-CP — CoT-CP is the calibration shell.
  - Step-level guarantee that does not silently degrade under prompt
    distribution shift, because q̂ has finite-sample validity under
    exchangeability.
  - Programming-vs-reasoning λ split disappears: a single calibration set
    per task family yields an automatic q̂.
- **Required experimental baseline**: yes — reproduce DEER on at least
  MATH-500 and AIME with R1-distill or QwQ-32B; report DEER's λ-tuned curve
  vs CoT-CP's α-targeted curve on the same axes.
- **Required citation**: §1 (Introduction — adaptive-exit baseline), §3
  (Related Work — confidence-gated step-level exit subsection), §5
  (Experiments — head-to-head row), §6 (Discussion — DEER's λ as a
  pre-conformal special case).

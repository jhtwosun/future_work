## Abstract (verbatim)

"Guaranteeing the correctness and factuality of language model (LM) outputs is a major open problem. In this work, we propose conformal factuality, a framework that can ensure high probability correctness guarantees for LMs by connecting language modeling and conformal prediction. Our insight is that the correctness of an LM output is equivalent to an uncertainty quantification problem, where the uncertainty sets are defined as the entailment set of an LM's output. Using this connection, we show that conformal prediction in language models corresponds to a back-off algorithm that provides high probability correctness guarantees by progressively making LM outputs less specific (and expanding the associated uncertainty sets). This approach applies to any black-box LM and requires very few human-annotated samples. Evaluations of our approach on closed book QA (FActScore, NaturalQuestions) and reasoning tasks (MATH) show that our approach can provide 80-90% correctness guarantees while retaining the majority of the LM's original output."

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

Mohri and Hashimoto recast LM correctness as a *set-cover* problem over the entailment closure of generated claims. They decompose a response into atomic subclaims, score each (e.g., self-eval probability), and use split CP to drop subclaims below a calibrated threshold; the surviving claim set is "less specific" but probably correct. The procedure is a back-off in entailment space: removing a claim is equivalent to weakening the response into a superset of admissible truths, so a single threshold gives a high-probability factuality guarantee on the residual answer. This is *the* foundational paper for claim-level CP and is the conceptual ancestor of Cherian-Gibbs-Candes (conditional version), Conformal-RAG, and Rubin-Toles (graph-structured version).

## Method
- **Conformity score**: per-claim correctness score (paper uses self-evaluated probability of correctness as the canonical example; framework allows any function on (X, claim)).
- **Calibration set**: prompts with atomic-claim-level correctness annotations (small human-labeled set; few hundred prompts in their experiments).
- **Coverage statement**: P[every retained subclaim is correct] >= 1 - alpha, marginal over (X, response). Equivalently, the back-off output is contained in the true entailment set with prob >= 1 - alpha.
- **Granularity**: atomic-claim level filtering inside a single response; output is a *subset* of the original generation, not a set of generations.

## Reported results
- Datasets: FActScore (biography factuality), NaturalQuestions (closed-book QA), MATH (reasoning).
- Models: black-box LMs (per the abstract; specific model list — typically GPT-3.5 / GPT-4 family in their tables — not transcribed here).
- Headline: 80-90% correctness guarantees retained while preserving "the majority" of original output content.
- The MATH evaluation is the directly relevant point of comparison for CoT-CP.

## How CoT-CP positions against this
- **Overlap with CoT-CP**: both target reasoning-time factuality via split CP at sub-response granularity. The Mohri-Hashimoto MATH evaluation is on the same modality as CoT-CP (chain-of-thought reasoning over math problems). Their atomic-claim filter is the closest semantic analog to a step-level filter.
- **What CoT-CP adds**:
  - Mohri-Hashimoto treats decomposed subclaims as *exchangeable, structurally independent* atoms; in CoT they explicitly drop ordering. CoT-CP's trajectory aggregator phi(R) = min_t S_t (or mean) is a *measurable function on the ordered chain*, preserving step structure (Theorem 1). This is the reasoning-flavored counterpart they explicitly do not handle.
  - Mohri-Hashimoto controls *retained-claim correctness*; CoT-CP controls *final-answer correctness conditional on keep* (selective accuracy). Different end-utility: theirs filters individual statements, ours decides whether to emit / abstain on the whole answer.
  - Score-family ladder. CoT-CP's Theorem 2 (LR+ Pareto) explicitly compares lp_min / prm_min / sc_top1 at fixed answer rate; Mohri-Hashimoto fixes one self-eval score and does not analyze cost.
  - Distribution shift. CoT-CP Theorem 3 (empirical-PMF weighted CP for discrete scores) handles MATH->AIME; Mohri-Hashimoto assumes fully exchangeable cal/test.
  - Step-level branching null result is a useful contrast: CoT-CP shows that *emitting alternatives* at a low-confidence step (the natural Mohri-Hashimoto-like "back-off into a more general claim" move at the step boundary) yields essentially zero gain over trajectory-level filtering across pilots C, K, L, M.

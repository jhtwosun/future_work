## Abstract (verbatim)

> Language models are increasingly being used in important decision pipelines, so ensuring the correctness of their outputs is crucial. Recent work has proposed evaluating the 'factuality' of claims decomposed from a language model generation and applying conformal prediction techniques to filter out those claims that are not factual. This can be effective for tasks such as information retrieval, where constituent claims may be evaluated in isolation for factuality, but is not appropriate for reasoning tasks, as steps of a logical argument can be evaluated for correctness only within the context of the claims that precede them. To capture this, we define 'coherent factuality' and develop a conformal-prediction-based method to guarantee coherent factuality for language model outputs. Our approach applies split conformal prediction to subgraphs within a 'deducibility graph' that represents the steps of a reasoning problem. We evaluate our method on mathematical reasoning problems from the MATH and FELM datasets and find that our algorithm consistently produces correct and substantiated orderings of claims, achieving coherent factuality across target coverage levels. Moreover, we achieve 90% factuality on our stricter definition while retaining 80% or more of the original claims, highlighting the utility of our deducibility-graph-guided approach.

## Key idea (3-5 sentences)

Rubin-Toles et al. argue that *atomic-claim factuality* (Mohri-Hashimoto, Cherian-Gibbs-Candès) is the wrong unit for *reasoning* outputs: a claim like "therefore x = 7" is true only conditional on the chain of derivations preceding it. They formalize this as **coherent factuality** — every retained claim is correct *and* substantiated by previously presented claims — and operationalize it by lifting CP from individual claims to *subgraphs* of a per-prompt **deducibility graph** whose nodes are claims and whose edges encode which claims justify which. Split CP is applied to score subgraphs (rather than isolated nodes), giving a coverage guarantee on the existence of a correct, substantiated ordering. They evaluate on math reasoning datasets MATH and FELM. This is the *first* paper to break the "claims are exchangeable atoms" assumption that all of Mohri-Hashimoto / Cherian-Gibbs-Candès / Conformal-RAG silently rely on, and is therefore the highest-conceptual-threat existing work to CoT-CP.

## Method
- **Conformity score**: a score over **subgraphs** of the deducibility graph, derived from per-claim self-eval probabilities composed across edges. Effectively a chain-conditional correctness score that respects the partial order of derivation.
- **Calibration set**: prompts with reasoning-trace ground truth; held-out subgraphs used to calibrate the split-CP threshold over subgraph scores.
- **Coverage statement**: marginal P[the retained subgraph satisfies coherent factuality, i.e., every retained claim is correct AND substantiated by retained predecessors] ≥ 1 − α. Strictly stronger than Mohri-Hashimoto's claim-wise correctness because it adds a structural deducibility constraint.
- **Granularity**: structured (subgraph) — a per-prompt ordered subset of claims, not a single claim or a full sequence.
- **Code**: https://github.com/maxrubintoles/Conformal_LM_Reasoning (open-source, suitable for direct baseline reproduction).

## Reported results
- **Datasets**: MATH and FELM (mathematical reasoning).
- **Headline**: 90% factuality on the stricter coherent-factuality definition while retaining ≥80% of the original claims, "across target coverage levels" (i.e., consistent across the α-grid).
- Specific per-α / per-model numbers and which LM backbones are used should be confirmed against the OpenReview PDF (forum AJpUZd8Clb) or arxiv `2505.17126` for the experimental table — not transcribed here.

## How CoT-CP positions against this
- **Overlap with CoT-CP**: very high — same domain (math reasoning), same goal (CP-calibrated selective output on a chain), same realization that claims-as-atoms is wrong for reasoning. Both are explicitly responses to the limitation of Mohri-Hashimoto. Both report at coverage / kept-rate trade-offs on math-reasoning datasets.
- **What CoT-CP adds**:
  - **Different unit of structure.** Rubin-Toles use a *deducibility graph* whose edges encode logical justification. CoT-CP uses a *linear ordered chain* with a step-aggregator φ(R) (e.g., min_t S_t). For most CoT outputs from current LMs the chain is a path (degenerate DAG), so CoT-CP's φ-aggregation is a *cheap, structure-free* alternative that does not require constructing or scoring a deducibility graph at all.
  - **Score-family ladder.** Rubin-Toles use a single self-eval-derived subgraph score. CoT-CP characterizes a Pareto frontier across `lp_min` (1×), `prm_min` (2×), `sc_top1` (8×) (Theorem 2, LR+ ordering). PRM as a mid-cost score is *new* here too.
  - **End-utility.** Rubin-Toles guarantee "every retained claim is correct AND deducible"; CoT-CP guarantees "kept answer is correct" (selective accuracy). Different target metric — final answer vs intermediate-claim coherence. For math problems the final answer is what users care about; for explanatory reasoning their guarantee is stronger.
  - **Inference cost.** Rubin-Toles must build and score a deducibility graph per prompt (multiple LM calls to elicit edges + per-subgraph scoring). CoT-CP `lp_min` is *free* given greedy decode; `prm_min` adds one PRM forward; only `sc_top1` matches their cost.
  - **Distribution shift.** Theorem 3 (empirical-PMF weighted CP for discrete scores under MATH→AIME shift) is something they do not address.
  - **Negative result.** Pilots C/K/L/M show step-level branching (the closest "follow-the-deducibility-graph and re-derive bad steps" extension) does *not* improve over trajectory CP, with budgeted experiments. Rubin-Toles' subgraph procedure can be read as a more sophisticated version of "find the bad step and replan"; our null result is a useful empirical caveat to their direction.
